### TL;DR
A ~200-line open-source markdown file from Anthropic's Claude desktop plugins—handling legal contract review that previously required billable paralegal hours—crystallized market fears about AI disruption, contributing to a ~$285 billion single-day selloff across software and financial services stocks.

### Key Quote
"the markdown file didn't cause this. It revealed it. The per-seat SaaS licensing model — the financial bedrock that the entire enterprise software economy has been built on for twenty years — was already cracking. The market just hadn't priced it in yet."

### Summary
- **Catalyst event**: On January 30th, Anthropic released plugins for Claude Coworker (desktop AI tool), including one for legal contract review that triages NDAs, flags non-standard clauses, and generates compliance summaries
- **What the plugin contained**: ~200 lines of structured markdown prompts—"first-year law school content" with workflow logic, open-source, with standard legal disclaimer
- **Market impact** (~$285B single-session collapse):
  - Thomson Reuters: -18% (biggest single-day decline on record)
  - RELX (LexisNexis parent): ~-14%
  - Wolters Kluwer: ~-13%
  - LegalZoom: ~-20%
  - FactSet: >-10%
  - Morningstar: -9%
  - Goldman Sachs U.S. software basket: worst session since April tariff selloff
- **Context**: Software companies were already missing revenue estimates at post-COVID correction rates; broader "AI disruption" repricing had been building
- **Author's thesis**: The markdown file revealed, rather than caused, structural vulnerability in per-seat SaaS licensing models
- **Named source**: Jeffrey Favuzza (Jefferies equity trading desk) coined "SaaSpocalypse" — described selling as "'get me out' style"
- **Teased but not delivered** (paywalled): Jensen Huang's counterargument and critique; KPMG/Big Four using AI as fee negotiation leverage; what survives vs. dies (data edge, accountability edge vs. per-seat model); "articulation problem" with build-your-own; fractal implications for knowledge workers
